Reflections

Creating a Webpage

Building this Intel Sustainability Timeline taught me how much structure matters in web development. Using HTML to lay out the content first — before touching any CSS — helped me understand how the browser reads a page from top to bottom. Flexbox was especially eye-opening: once I understood that flex-direction and media queries work together, making the timeline switch from horizontal to vertical on smaller screens felt intuitive rather than confusing. I also learned that small details like transitions and hover effects are what make a page feel polished and interactive, not just static.

Networking

This project made me think about how sharing work publicly — even a simple webpage — is itself a form of networking. Developers constantly share projects on GitHub, Codepen, and portfolios to show what they can build. Intel's sustainability page is a real example of a company using the web to communicate values and build relationships with stakeholders. As I build more projects, I want to get in the habit of documenting and sharing them, because the work itself opens conversations with other developers and potential collaborators.

Company Culture

Researching Intel's RISE strategy gave me a different view of what company culture can look like at a large technology corporation. Setting public, measurable goals — like net-zero emissions by 2030 or restoring more water than consumed — signals that sustainability isn't just marketing but an accountability structure. This kind of culture affects who the company attracts: engineers and designers who care about the real-world impact of their work. It made me reflect on what I'd want from a workplace: not just interesting technical problems, but a sense that the work connects to something larger.
